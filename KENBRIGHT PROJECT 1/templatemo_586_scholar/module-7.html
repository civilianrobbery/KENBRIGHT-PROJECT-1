7.2 Fulfilment Cash Flows

At initial recognition, an entity is required to measure a group of insurance contracts as the sum of:

Fulfilment Cash Flows (FCF)

Includes:

  • Estimates of future cash inflows and outflows
  • A discount adjustment to reflect the time value of money and financial risks
  • A risk adjustment for non-financial uncertainty
Contractual Service Margin (CSM)

Represents the unearned profit from the contracts

Key Components
Fulfilment Cash Flows
  • Risk-adjusted present value of future cashflows (premiums, claims)
  • Includes discount adjustment and risk adjustment
Contractual Service Margin
  • Represents unearned profit for the insurer
  • Ensures no day-one profit recognition

7.3 Discounting

This sub-module introduces the key principle of discounting in IFRS 17, highlighting the need to adjust future cash flows to account for the time value of money. This process ensures that projected future payments and receipts are presented in today's terms, leading to a more accurate valuation of insurance liabilities.

Regulatory Framework
Paragraph Requirement
Paragraph 38 Includes discounting as part of the fulfilment cash flows
Paragraphs 38–39 Outline the key characteristics that discount rates must reflect
Paragraphs 44(b) and 47 Explain how interest is accreted on the CSM using the original discount rate set at contract inception
Appendix B72–B85 Provides detailed guidance on selecting appropriate discount rates and constructing yield curves
What is Discounting?

Discounting is the process of converting future cash flows into present values based on the principle that a sum of money held now is more valuable than the same amount received later — this is known as the time value of money.

The discount rate used should capture features such as liquidity, inflation expectations, and how the cash flows are influenced by any underlying items.

Present Value = Future Cash Flow / (1 + r)^n Where: r = discount rate n = number of periods
Why Are Future Cash Flows Discounted in IFRS 17?

To reflect the time value of money and financial risks not already captured in the estimates.

Purpose
  • Ensure liabilities are shown realistically on the balance sheet
  • Helps compare cash flows that occur at different times
  • Adjusts for liquidity and financial risk

7.4 Discounting Approaches

IFRS 17 outlines two methods insurers can use to determine appropriate discount rates:

a) Bottom-Up Approach

How It Works:

  • Begins with a risk-free yield curve (such as government bond rates)
  • Adds a liquidity premium if the insurance contracts are not easily tradable (i.e., illiquid)
Discount Rate = Risk-Free Rate + Liquidity Premium
b) Top-Down Approach

How It Works:

  • Starts with the total yield of a reference asset portfolio
  • Eliminates components not relevant to insurance (e.g., credit risk, other market risks)
Discount Rate = Portfolio Yield - Credit Risk - Other Irrelevant Risks
Market Consistency Requirement: IFRS 17 requires that discount rates exclude market variables that don't affect the cash flows of the insurance contract, even if these variables are reflected in market prices. This ensures accuracy in aligning economic reality with liability measurement.
Key Discounting Concepts in IFRS 17
Concept Description
Time Value of Money Money today is more valuable than money tomorrow
Discount Rate The rate used to bring future cash flows to the present value
Bottom-Up Approach Start with a risk-free rate, then adjust for liquidity
Top-Down Approach Start with a portfolio yield, then remove credit and other risks not relevant to the insurance contract
Liquidity Premium Adjustment made to a liquid risk-free yield curve to reflect differences between liquidity characteristics of the financial instruments that underlie the risk-free rates and insurance contracts
Market Consistency Use observable market data only if it reflects the characteristics of the liability

7.5 Contractual Service Margin

This sub-module provides a comprehensive overview of how the CSM is calculated, adjusted, and released under IFRS 17.

Regulatory Framework
Paragraph What It Covers

44–45 CSM changes: interest, release, updates to cash flows
46 Onerous contracts: CSM set to zero, loss component created

B84–B96 Allocation of CSM to coverage periods

What Is the Contractual Service Margin (CSM)?

CSM represents the unearned profit an insurance company expects to earn as it provides coverage over the life of an insurance contract. It is part of the Liability for Remaining Coverage (LRC).

Analogy: Imagine selling a 4-year gym membership today. The full fee is paid upfront, but you haven't provided the service yet. The profit you're expecting to make is spread over the 4 years – that's your CSM.

7.6 CSM at Initial Recognition

When a group of insurance contracts is first recognised (usually at the point of inception), IFRS 17 requires calculating a "Contractual Service Margin (CSM)" if the contract is expected to be profitable.

Calculation Formula:
CSM = Present Value of Future Inflows - (Present Value of Future Outflows + Risk Adjustment)

7.7 CSM at Subsequent Measurement

Once the initial CSM is set up, it's not static. It gets adjusted over time to reflect:

Interest Accretion

CSM increases over time using the discount rate at initial recognition

Profit Release (reduction)

CSM is released as revenue as the insurer provides coverage. Usually done evenly unless another pattern better reflects the service

Adjustments for Changes in Estimates

If assumptions about future cash flows change (e.g., fewer claims expected), the CSM is adjusted, but only if the contract is not onerous

Onerous Contracts

If the group becomes loss-making: CSM is set to zero. A loss component is recognised for the shortfall

CSM Adjustment Formula:
CSM(t) = CSM(t-1) × (1 + r) - Release + Adjustments Where: r = locked-in discount rate Release = coverage-based amortization Adjustments = changes in future service FCF
Loss Component Reversal: A loss component can be reversed in subsequent periods if there are favorable changes in the fulfilment cash flows related to future service, indicating that the group of contracts is no longer onerous.

7.8 Risk Adjustment

This sub-module focuses on the Risk Adjustment, a critical element in calculating insurance contract liabilities under IFRS 17.

Regulatory Framework
Paragraph Requirement
Paragraph 37 Identifies the Risk Adjustment as a part of the fulfilment cash flows
Paragraph 44 Requires disclosure of the methods used and the confidence level (or equivalent)
Appendix B86–B92 Provides detailed guidance on its principles, methodologies, and influencing factors
What is Risk Adjustment in IFRS 17?

The Risk Adjustment for non-financial risk represents the amount an insurer charges for bearing the uncertainty around timing and amount of future cash flows, stemming from risks such as mortality, morbidity, lapse, and expenses.

Purpose: While expected cash flows capture the average outcome, the risk adjustment reflects variability and uncertainty around those expectations. It ensures liabilities include not just a neutral estimate, but also a margin that aligns with the insurer's risk appetite and capacity to bear risk.

7.9 Factors That Influence the Risk Adjustment

The Risk Adjustment for non-financial risk reflects the level of compensation the insurer requires for bearing the uncertainty around cash flows. According to IFRS 17 Appendix B88–B91, key influencing factors include:

Degree of Uncertainty

Greater uncertainty in the timing or amount of future cash flows leads to a higher risk adjustment

Duration of Contracts

Longer coverage periods increase the risk exposure over time, resulting in a higher risk adjustment due to more future uncertainty

Amount of Claims

Higher potential variability in future claim amounts contributes to a greater need for compensation

Policyholder Characteristics & Options

Contracts with features such as lapse options, guarantees, or uncertain policyholder behaviour increase complexity and raise risk adjustment

Quality of Data and Estimates

Less credible or incomplete data increases estimation uncertainty, resulting in a higher risk adjustment

Diversification

Portfolios with higher diversification benefits may require lower aggregate risk adjustments, as risks offset each other

Reinsurance Impact

When risk is transferred through reinsurance, the risk adjustment is recognized separately for the cost and the reinsurer, reflecting the risk retained and assumed

Confidence Level Considerations

Different confidence levels represent varying degrees of risk coverage and cost implications:

75% Confidence Interval
  • Provides moderate coverage of obligations, balancing risk and cost
  • Lower risk adjustment, lower cost
90% Confidence Interval
  • Offers high coverage, ensuring most obligations are met but at a higher cost
  • Higher risk adjustment, greater protection

7.10 Methods of Determining Risk Adjustment

IFRS 17 does not mandate a specific calculation method for the risk adjustment. However, it sets out the principles that any method must meet:

Required Principles
  • The RA must reflect the compensation an entity requires for bearing uncertainty from non-financial risks
  • It must be explicitly and separately disclosed from other fulfilment cash flow components
  • The method chosen must be consistent with the entity's own risk assessment — it should reflect how the entity would internally price and manage non-financial risk
Common Approaches
1. Confidence Level Approach (Quantile Method)
  • Sets the Risk Adjustment based on a selected confidence level
  • Determines the value of future cash flows such that the entity is 75% or 90% confident it can meet its obligations
RA = Value at Risk(confidence level) - Expected Value
2. Cost of Capital Method
  • Estimates the RA as the cost of holding capital to support non-financial risk over the lifetime of the contract
  • Components: Risk capital amount, capital holding period, cost of capital rate (e.g., 6%)
RA = Σ [Capital Requirement_t × Cost of Capital Rate × (1 + r)^{-t}]
3. Conditional Tail Expectation (CTE)
  • Also known as Tail Value at Risk
  • Looks at the average loss beyond a selected confidence level, capturing extreme outcomes
RA = E[Loss | Loss > Value at Risk(confidence level)] - Expected Value

7.11 Integrated Measurement Framework

Complete Insurance Contract Measurement
Insurance Contract Liability = Fulfilment Cash Flows + Contractual Service Margin Where: Fulfilment Cash Flows = Present Value(Future Cash Flows) + Risk Adjustment
Measurement Relationships
Integration Principles
  • Discounting affects present value of both cash flows and CSM interest accretion
  • Risk Adjustment directly impacts CSM calculation at initial recognition
  • CSM ensures systematic profit recognition aligned with service delivery
  • All components must be consistently measured and regularly updated
Comprehensive Approach: This integrated framework ensures that insurance contracts are measured comprehensively, reflecting both the economic reality of the obligations and the appropriate timing of profit recognition.
