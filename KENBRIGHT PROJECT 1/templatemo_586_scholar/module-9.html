9.1 Introduction

This module provides a comprehensive overview of the Premium Allocation Approach (PAA) as per IFRS 17 paragraphs 53 to 59.

The Premium Allocation Approach (PAA) is a simplified method for measuring insurance contract liabilities under IFRS 17. It is primarily applied to short-term contracts—usually those with coverage periods of 12 months or less—such as motor, travel, health, and various general insurance policies.

Conceptually, the PAA resembles the unearned premium reserve (UPR) approach previously used under IFRS 4.

Portfolio Grouping Requirements
Grouping Requirements
  • Contracts must be grouped into portfolios with similar risk characteristics and management practices
  • Each portfolio is classified into:
    • Onerous at inception
    • No significant risk of becoming onerous
    • Profitable contracts
  • Contracts issued more than 12 months apart cannot be grouped together

9.2 Eligibility Criteria

According to the standard, PAA can be applied under these two conditions:

Condition A: Coverage Period

If the coverage period of each contract in the group is one year or less

Condition B: Materiality Assessment

If the entity reasonably expects that using the PAA would produce a measurement of the liability for remaining coverage (LRC) that does not differ materially from the General Measurement Model (GMM)

9.3 Measurement Under PAA

There are two primary components of liability to consider:

A. Liability for Remaining Coverage (LRC)

This reflects the portion of premiums that pertains to future periods of coverage, adjusted for acquisition costs.

Components of LRC Calculation:
  • Opening LRC balance: The starting balance at the beginning of the period
  • Add: Premium Received: Additional premiums collected during the period
  • Less: Amortization of insurance time value of money and financial risks: Amortized acquisition costs deducted
  • Less: Insurance Revenue: Revenue recognized over time as insurance services are provided
B. Liability for Incurred Claims (LIC)

This is measured in a manner similar to the General Measurement Model (GMM):

  • Present value of future cash flows
  • Risk adjustment for non-financial risk
LRC Formula
LRC = Opening LRC + Premium received – Earned Premium – Change in Deferred Acquisition Costs (DAC)
Where Premium received = Gross Written Premium (GWP) + Prior premium receivables – Current premium receivables

9.4 Treatment of Acquisition Cash Flows

Entities have the option to either expense acquisition costs immediately when the coverage period is one year or less, or to defer and amortize these costs over the coverage period if it exceeds one year.

Accounting Policy Choices
  • Immediate Expense: When coverage period is one year or less
  • Defer and Amortize: When coverage period exceeds one year
Important Consideration: Deferring acquisition costs reduces the Liability for Remaining Coverage (LRC), but this may lead to a higher recognition of losses for onerous contracts.

9.5 Discounting and Risk Adjustment

Discounting Treatment
  • Discounting of the LRC is not required unless there is a significant delay between receipt of premiums and the provision of insurance services
  • Simplified approach reduces complexity for short-term contracts
Risk Adjustment Application
  • Risk adjustment applies solely to the Liability for Incurred Claims (LIC)
  • Not applied to the Liability for Remaining Coverage (LRC)
  • Focuses on uncertainty in claim settlement timing and amounts

9.6 Onerous Contracts Under PAA

Even under PAA, entities must assess whether contracts are onerous at initial recognition or subsequently.

Critical Requirement: If a contract is deemed onerous, a loss component must be recognized immediately in profit or loss.
Onerous Contract Assessment
Assessment Points
  • Initial recognition evaluation
  • Subsequent measurement reviews
  • Continuous monitoring throughout coverage period
  • Immediate loss recognition for onerous contracts

9.7 Revenue Recognition

Revenue is recognized over the coverage period in line with the pattern of transfer of services (typically straight-line).

Recognition Pattern
  • Typically straight-line over coverage period
  • Aligned with service provision pattern
  • Systematic and rational allocation
Timing Considerations
  • Matches revenue with service delivery
  • Reflects coverage period duration
  • Consistent application across portfolio

Complexity Low High

Discounting Not required for LRC Required

9.9 Disclosure Requirements

IFRS 17 mandates the following disclosures:

Mandatory Disclosures
  • A transparent presentation of revenue, incurred claims, and movements in insurance liabilities
  • Disclosure of confidence levels used in measuring liabilities
Optional Presentation
  • An option to present changes in discount rates through Other Comprehensive Income (OCI)

9.10 Practical Application Examples

Ideal Applications
  • Group life insurance
  • Group credit insurance
  • General insurance with short coverage periods
  • Reinsurance contracts held (meeting eligibility criteria)

9.11 Key Implementation Considerations

Portfolio Segmentation
  • Group contracts with similar risk characteristics
  • Separate profitable from onerous contracts
  • Maintain temporal consistency (within 12-month issuance window)
Measurement Frequency
  • Regular assessment of LRC and LIC
  • Continuous monitoring of contract profitability
  • Periodic review of eligibility criteria
Transition Provisions
  • Appropriate transition from IFRS 4 UPR approach
  • Consistent application across all eligible contracts
  • Proper documentation of materiality assessments

9.12 Conclusion: Mastering Premium Allocation Approach

The Premium Allocation Approach provides a simplified yet comprehensive framework for measuring insurance contracts with short duration. By understanding the eligibility criteria, measurement components, and disclosure requirements, entities can effectively implement PAA while maintaining compliance with IFRS 17 standards.

Key Takeaways:
  • PAA is designed for short-term contracts (≤12 months) or when materially similar to GMM
  • Liability for Remaining Coverage reflects unearned premiums adjusted for acquisition costs
  • Liability for Incurred Claims follows GMM principles with risk adjustment
  • Onerous contract assessment remains mandatory under PAA
  • Proper portfolio grouping and consistent application are critical for successful implementation
