11.1 Introduction

This module provides an overview on the treatment of investment contracts with discretionary participation features (DPF) under IFRS 17.

Investment contracts with discretionary participation features are financial instruments where investors receive additional benefits, in addition to guaranteed amounts, based on the issuer's discretion and the performance of a specified pool of assets or contracts. These benefits are not fixed and are determined by the issuer, impacting the overall investment return.

Key Characteristics:
  • These contracts can contain both insurance-related and non-insurance elements
  • Valuation requires considerable judgment due to discretionary nature of cash flows
  • Variable Fee Approach (VFA) may apply where eligible
  • Underlying items must be explicitly defined and unchangeable after contract inception
Consistency in Accounting Approach

Participating investment contracts and participating insurance contracts are often backed by the same underlying pool of assets. Applying a consistent accounting approach to both provides more meaningful and comparable information to financial statement users while also reducing complexity.

Shared Features: Both types of contracts frequently share key features such as long durations, recurring premium payments, and high acquisition costs, despite different classifications.

11.2 Definition and Scope

What is an Investment Contract with Discretionary Participation Features (DPF)?

An Investment Contract with DPF is a financial contract that:

Definition Criteria
  • Does not transfer significant insurance risk
  • Gives policyholder contractual right to receive additional benefits that are:
    • Significant in value
    • Discretionary (issuer has freedom in determining them)
    • Linked to performance of a pool of underlying items
Scope Requirement: IFRS 17 requires that such contracts be accounted for, but with some modifications, only if the issuer also issues insurance contracts.

11.3 Regulatory Framework

IFRS 17 References for Investment Contracts with DPFs
Reference Section Content Summary
Para 3(c) Scope IFRS 17 applies to investment contracts with DPFs if issued by insurers (i.e., entities also issuing insurance contracts)
Appendix A Definitions Defines investment contracts with DPFs and sets three key criteria: discretion, significance, and performance linkage
Para 71 Recognition & Measurement Modifications for DPFs: Recognize when entity becomes party to contract; Contract boundary based on substantive investment services; CSM allocated to reflect investment service
BC83-BC86 Basis for Conclusions Justifies including investment contracts with DPFs under IFRS 17: Economic substance similar to insurance contracts; Ensures consistent accounting treatment
Para 88-89 Contractual Service Margin Explains systematic allocation of CSM for DPF contracts based on transfer of investment service
Para B73-B75 Implementation Guidance Provides guidance on investment services and how to allocate CSM accordingly

11.4 Examples of Investment Contracts with Discretionary Participation Features

1. With-profits Savings Policies
  • Offers bonuses tied to fund performance
  • No insurance coverage
  • Bonuses based on fund performance, no life cover
2. Deferred Annuities (Investment Only)
  • Value grows over time
  • Discretionary bonuses apply, but no guaranteed returns or insurance elements
  • Accumulates value, bonuses discretionary, no guarantees
3. Participating Deposit Contracts
  • Provide a basic return plus a discretionary bonus derived from company profits
  • Base return + discretionary top-up from profits
4. Group Savings Plans
  • Contributions pooled and invested
  • Participants receive discretionary bonuses based on investment outcomes
  • Employees share in pooled investment performance; bonuses are discretionary

11.5 Comparison: Direct Participation Contracts vs Investment Contracts with DPFs

Direct Participation Contracts (DPCs)
  • Definition: Insurance contract where policyholder shares in performance of clearly identified underlying items
  • Standard: Fully within scope of IFRS 17, using Variable Fee Approach (VFA)
  • Purpose: Combines insurance coverage with investment return sharing
Investment Contracts with DPFs
  • Definition: Financial contract (not insurance) giving right to discretionary returns based on asset performance
  • Standard: Within scope of IFRS 17 (with modifications), only if issuer also issues insurance contracts
  • Purpose: Provides investment-like benefits with discretionary bonuses, but no insurance risk
Key Characteristics Comparison
Feature Direct Participation Contracts (DPCs) Investment Contracts with DPFs
Insurance Risk Present? ✓ Yes ✗ No
Participation in Underlying Items? ✓ Yes (clearly identified) ✓ Yes
Discretionary Participation? ✓ Yes ✓ Yes
Measurement Approach Variable Fee Approach (VFA) under IFRS 17 General Model with modifications under IFRS 17
Eligibility Criteria Must meet 3 conditions: share in underlying items, substantial share of fair value returns, payments vary with fair value changes Must provide discretionary returns + issued by insurer that also issues insurance contracts
Discounting Uses current rates for cash flows not based on underlying items Similar, but less focus on variability in fees
CSM Adjustment for Financial Risk? ✓ Yes, using current interest rates ✗ No, uses locked-in rate unless modified

11.6 Accounting Treatment Components

Recognition

The process of acknowledging DPF contracts in financial statements

  • Recognize when entity becomes party to contract
  • Contract boundary based on substantive investment services
Measurement

Determining net financial value of DPF contracts

  • Uses General Measurement Model with modifications
  • CSM allocated to reflect investment service
Presentation

How DPF contracts are displayed in financial records

  • Separate presentation from insurance contracts
  • Clear disclosure of discretionary participation features
Key Concepts
Variable Fee Approach

A method where fees vary with asset returns. Applicable for eligible contracts where entity's fee varies with underlying asset returns. Treats insurer's compensation as variable amount tied to returns from underlying assets.

Underlying Items

Assets or indices that influence contract payouts. Items that determine some of the amounts payable to a policyholder. Can include reference portfolio, net assets, or external indices. Must be fixed and clearly identified—retrospective changes violate requirements.

11.7 Key Implementation Requirements

Contract Boundaries
  • Based on substantive investment services provided
  • Must reflect nature of investment service rather than insurance coverage
CSM Allocation
  • Systematic allocation based on transfer of investment service
  • Reflects pattern of investment services provided
  • Amortized over the coverage period
Disclosure Requirements
  • Nature and extent of discretionary participation features
  • Basis for determining discretionary benefits
  • Impact on financial position and performance
  • Significant judgments and estimates used

11.8 Critical Success Factors

Essential Requirements for Successful Implementation
Data Requirements
  • Clear identification of underlying items
  • Historical data on discretionary benefit payments
  • Performance tracking of reference portfolios
Judgment Areas
  • Assessment of significance of discretionary benefits
  • Determination of substantive investment services
  • Estimation of future discretionary payments
System Capabilities
  • Ability to track underlying item performance
  • CSM calculation and allocation mechanisms
  • Disclosure compilation and reporting

11.9 Conclusion: Mastering Investment Contracts with DPF

Module 11 provides comprehensive guidance on accounting for investment contracts with discretionary participation features under IFRS 17. These contracts represent a unique category that bridges investment and insurance accounting, requiring careful assessment of discretionary elements and proper application of modified measurement approaches.

Key Takeaways:
  • DPF contracts provide discretionary benefits linked to underlying item performance without significant insurance risk
  • Scope applies only when issuer also issues insurance contracts
  • Clear differentiation required between DPCs (insurance) and DPFs (investment)
  • General Measurement Model with modifications applies to DPF contracts
  • Proper implementation requires robust data, sound judgment, and capable systems
  • Comprehensive disclosures ensure transparency in discretionary benefit arrangements
