13.1 Introduction

This module provides an in-depth overview of the fundamental changes in the presentation of the statement of financial position under IFRS 17 compared to IFRS 4, as covered under paragraphs 78-82 of the standard. The transition from IFRS 4 to IFRS 17 represents a significant shift in how insurance companies present their financial position, moving from diverse practices to a standardized, transparent approach that enhances comparability across insurers and jurisdictions.

Standardization Objective: IFRS 17 mandates that insurance companies present insurance and reinsurance contracts separately in their financial statements with clear distinction between assets and liabilities. This separation requirement ensures that stakeholders can clearly understand the financial exposure and position of the insurer.

13.2 Balance Sheet Presentation Requirements

Under IFRS 17, insurers must clearly present in their statement of financial position:

Insurance Contracts Issued - Assets

Contracts where expected inflows exceed outflows

Insurance Contracts Issued - Liabilities

Contracts where expected outflows exceed inflows

Reinsurance Contracts Held - Assets

Reinsurance recoverables and related assets

Reinsurance Contracts Held - Liabilities

Reinsurance obligations and related liabilities

Fundamental Departure:

This explicit separation represents a fundamental departure from IFRS 4, where net presentation was common and often obscured the true financial position of insurers.

Key Balance Sheet Principles:
Liability Recognition

If a group of insurance contracts is expected to pay out more than it will receive (i.e., expected outflows exceed inflows), it must be shown as a liability. This applies equally to:

  • Onerous contracts (those expected to generate losses)
  • Outstanding claims (amounts the insurer still owes to policyholders)
Acquisition Costs Treatment

Acquisition costs, such as commissions paid to agents and brokers, are included in the total value of contracts on the balance sheet rather than being presented separately as deferred acquisition costs (DAC). This integration provides a more comprehensive picture of the insurer's financial position by reflecting these costs within the contract measurement.

13.3 Income Statement Presentation

In the statement of profit or loss (income statement), IFRS 17 requires clear presentation of two main components:

1. Insurance Service Result

This represents income and expenses from providing insurance services, excluding investment-related amounts.

Components Included:
  • Premiums earned from providing insurance coverage
  • Claims and benefits paid during the period
  • Acquisition costs allocated to the period
  • Changes in risk adjustment for non-financial risk
  • Amortization of the contractual service margin (CSM)
2. Insurance Finance Income or Expenses

This component reflects the effect of interest rates and time on expected future cash flows.

Components Included:
  • Time value of money on future cash flows
  • Changes in discount rates
  • Financial risks associated with insurance contracts
Risk Adjustment Treatment:

An insurer is not required to disaggregate the change in the risk adjustment for non-financial risk between the insurance service result and insurance finance income or expenses. If the insurer elects not to make such a disaggregation, the entire change in the risk adjustment for non-financial risk must be included as part of the insurance service result.

Ongoing Review Requirement

IFRS 17 requires insurers to review their estimates and assumptions at each reporting date to ensure they continue to reflect the true financial situation, incorporating current market conditions and updated expectations.

13.4 IFRS 4 vs IFRS 17: Comprehensive Comparison

The table below provides a detailed comparison of presentation requirements between IFRS 4 and IFRS 17 across critical financial reporting aspects:

Aspect IFRS 4 IFRS 17
Presentation on Insurance Contracts Insurers often presented net positions (e.g., premiums receivable net of claims payable), leading to transparency issues in financial exposure assessment. No specific rules mandated separate presentation. Requires clear separation between insurance contract assets and insurance contract liabilities. Ensures clarity on whether an entity is in a net asset or net liability position for each contract group.
Level of Aggregation Presentation typically occurred at portfolio or product line level with limited grouping guidance, resulting in inconsistent practices across the industry. Contracts must be grouped by issue year and expected profitability into: (i) onerous contracts, (ii) contracts with no significant possibility of becoming onerous, and (iii) remaining contracts. Presentation occurs at the contract group level.
Contractual Service Margin (CSM) CSM was not recognized. Profits were often front-loaded or deferred through unearned premium reserves or deferred acquisition costs (DAC). CSM is a core liability component representing unearned profit. It is amortized over the coverage period, ensuring profit recognition aligns with service provision.
Acquisition Costs (DAC) Acquisition costs could be capitalized and presented as a separate Deferred Acquisition Cost asset, disconnected from insurance liabilities. Acquisition costs are included in fulfillment cash flows, which affect the CSM. DAC is no longer a separate balance sheet item, aligning cost recognition with insurance liability measurement.
Onerous Contracts No explicit requirement existed to identify or account separately for onerous contracts, allowing insurers to smooth losses over time. Onerous contract groups must be identified at initial recognition. Any loss is recognized immediately in profit or loss, and the group is shown as a liability in the statement of financial position.
Risk Adjustment Not required. No standardized method existed to reflect uncertainty or risk in liability valuation, leading to inconsistent risk reporting. A risk adjustment for non-financial risk must be included in insurance liability measurement. This adds a buffer for uncertainty in fulfillment cash flows, enhancing risk transparency.
Reinsurance Contracts Often netted against underlying insurance contracts or presented inconsistently across companies, reducing comparability. Reinsurance contracts are presented separately as assets or liabilities and measured independently from underlying insurance contracts, improving transparency.
Transparency & Comparability Low comparability across insurers due to different local GAAPs and inconsistent application. Diverse presentations reduced financial statement usefulness. High comparability through uniform structure enforcement. Enhanced disclosures improve comparability across insurers and jurisdictions.
Measurement Basis Frequently relied on historical assumptions, such as unadjusted premium cash flows and static reserves. Assumptions were not regularly updated. Based on current estimates of future cash flows, updated at each reporting date. Includes changes in assumptions and discounting, improving relevance and reliability.
Offsetting Allowed? Offsetting was common (e.g., premiums receivable net of claims payable), reducing visibility of gross exposures and true financial position. Offsetting is prohibited. This rule enhances balance sheet clarity by requiring gross presentation of assets and liabilities.

Key Structural Changes in Balance Sheet Presentation:
Assets Restructuring
  • Elimination of separate "Deferred acquisition costs" line item
  • Consolidation of insurance-related receivables into "Insurance contract assets"
  • Clear separation between "Insurance contract assets" and "Reinsurance contract assets"
Liabilities Reorganization
  • Removal of "Deferred Commission Income" as a separate liability
  • Consolidation of insurance contract liabilities into a single comprehensive line item
  • Separate presentation of reinsurance contract liabilities
  • Elimination of detailed payable classifications related to insurance and reinsurance arrangements
Enhanced Classification
  • Clear distinction between insurance and reinsurance contracts
  • Integrated presentation of all contract-related assets and liabilities
  • Improved aggregation following the contract grouping requirements

13.6 Key Takeaways and Implementation Implications

Enhanced Transparency
  • IFRS 17 requires clear separation of insurance assets and liabilities
  • Eliminates net presentation common under IFRS 4
  • Enables better assessment of insurer's true financial exposure
Acquisition Costs Integration
  • Deferred Acquisition Costs (DAC) absorbed into fulfillment cash flows
  • No longer presented as separate assets
  • Aligns cost recognition with insurance liability recognition
Onerous Contract Recognition
  • Onerous contracts explicitly identified and measured
  • Losses recognized immediately rather than deferred
  • Enhanced disclosure requirements
Offsetting Prohibition
  • Prohibition on offsetting receivables and liabilities
  • Enhances balance sheet clarity
  • Provides better insight into scale of insurance operations
Risk and Profitability Alignment
  • Risk adjustments and CSM significantly alter liability presentation
  • Profit recognition aligns with service provision
  • Moves away from premium collection timing
Transition Impact

The transition from IFRS 4 to IFRS 17 requires significant restructuring of balance sheet presentation, with particular attention to:

1
Reclassification of insurance and reinsurance balances

Restructuring existing balances to align with new presentation requirements

2
Integration of acquisition costs into contract measurements

Eliminating separate DAC presentation and integrating costs into liability measurement

3
Implementation of new grouping and aggregation requirements

Establishing contract groups by issue year and profitability status

4
Establishment of CSM and risk adjustment calculations

Developing methodologies for CSM amortization and risk adjustment measurement

5
Development of robust disclosure frameworks

Creating comprehensive disclosures to support the new presentation format

Comprehensive Transformation: This comprehensive approach to financial statement presentation under IFRS 17 fundamentally enhances the transparency, comparability, and decision-usefulness of insurance company financial reporting.

13.7 Conclusion: Mastering Financial Statement Presentation

Module 13 establishes the comprehensive framework for financial statement presentation under IFRS 17, highlighting the fundamental shift from IFRS 4's diverse practices to a standardized, transparent approach. The enhanced presentation requirements ensure stakeholders can clearly understand an insurer's financial position, risk exposure, and performance drivers.

Key Implementation Focus Areas:
  • Separate presentation of insurance and reinsurance contract assets and liabilities
  • Clear distinction between insurance service results and insurance finance income/expenses
  • Integration of acquisition costs into contract measurements
  • Prohibition of offsetting to enhance balance sheet clarity
  • Implementation of robust disclosure frameworks supporting the new presentation
