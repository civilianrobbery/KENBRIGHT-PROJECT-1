8.1 Introduction

This module provides an in-depth overview of onerous contracts, as set out in paragraphs 47-52 of IFRS 17.

An onerous contract is an insurance contract (or group of contracts) where expected costs (including claims, expenses, and risk margin) exceed the premiums received i.e. cash outflows are greater than cash inflows.

Scope: Covers IFRS 17 paragraphs 47-52 on onerous contract identification and treatment.

8.2 At Initial Recognition

A contract (or group of contracts) is onerous at initial recognition if the expected outflows (claims, expenses, risk margin + acquisition costs) exceed inflows (premiums).

If contracts are onerous at initial recognition, IFRS 17 requires the insurer to:

Separate Grouping

Group them separately from profitable contracts

Immediate Loss Recognition

Recognize loss immediately in the profit and loss

CSM Zeroization

Set the Contractual Service Margin (CSM) to zero

Full Liability Recognition

Recognize full expected cost as a liability

Loss Component Creation

Establish a Loss Component to track the loss

Initial Recognition Decision Process
Initial Recognition ↓ [Calculate Fulfilment Cash Flows] ↓ [FCF > Expected Premiums] ----> [Contract is Onerous] ↓ ↓ [FCF ≤ Expected Premiums] [Group Separately] ↓ ↓ [Establish CSM] [Set CSM to Zero] ↓ ↓ [Recognize Loss in P&L] ↓ ↓ [Create Loss Component]

8.3 At Subsequent Measurement

A group of contracts becomes onerous, or more onerous, after initial recognition if:

Triggering Events
  • The expected future costs or claims increase, e.g. worse assumptions
  • For participation contracts, the expected investment returns decrease

If this happens:

Loss Recognition

Recognize additional loss in P&L

CSM Utilization

Use any remaining CSM to offset the loss

Loss Component Adjustment

Increase or add the Loss Component in the liability

Subsequent Measurement Process
Subsequent Measurement ↓ [Review Assumptions] ↓ [Costs Increase OR Investment Returns Decrease] ↓ [Calculate Additional Loss] ↓ [Use Remaining CSM to Absorb Loss] ↓ [Recognize Excess Loss in P&L] ↓ [Establish/Increase Loss Component]

8.4 Loss Component

The loss component is a part of the liability for remaining coverage that represents the recognized losses in the profit or loss.

Role of the Loss Component:
Loss Capture

Holds the loss recognized in the P&L

Gradual Release

Is released gradually as coverage is provided

Adjustment Mechanism

Is reduced if future assumptions improve

CSM Constraint

The CSM can only increase after the loss component is fully reversed

Loss Component Mechanics
Loss Component ↓ [Captures Recognized Losses] ↓ [Released Over Coverage Period] ↓ [Reduced with Favorable Changes] ↓ [Blocks CSM Increase Until Cleared]

8.5 Assessing Onerous Contracts and Impact of Assumption Changes

When assumptions such as future claims, operating costs, or risk margins change, the resulting impact is split proportionally between the loss component and the remaining part of the liability for remaining coverage.

This approach ensures that the loss component is gradually reduced and fully released by the end of the contract's coverage duration.

Assumption Change Allocation
Assumption Changes ↓ [Calculate Total Impact] ↓ [Proportional Allocation] ↓ [Loss Component] ←---→ [Remaining Liability] ↓ ↓ [Gradual Release] [Coverage Period] ↓ ↓ [End of Coverage] ←---→ [Fully Released]

8.6 Combined Ratio Assessment

An insurer will classify a group of insurance contracts as onerous if the projected combined ratio for the portfolio goes above 100%. The combined ratio is determined as follows:

Combined Ratio = (Insurance Service Expenses / Insurance Revenue) × 100
Insurance Service Expenses Include:
Expense Components
  • Incurred claims and other directly attributable outflows
  • Adjustments made to the Liability for Incurred Claims (LIC)
  • Recognized losses related to onerous contracts
  • Amortization of insurance acquisition cash flows

In making this assessment, insurers also review combined ratios from earlier cohorts. This historical view helps provide context and identify trends in portfolio performance.

Onerous Contract Identification Framework
Portfolio Analysis ↓ [Calculate Combined Ratio] ↓ [Ratio > 100%] ----> [Contract is Onerous] ↓ [Ratio ≤ 100%] ----> [Contract is Profitable] ↓ [Review Historical Cohorts] ↓ [Identify Performance Trends]

8.7 Key Treatment Summary

Initial Recognition Treatment
Action Requirement Purpose
Separate Grouping Group separately from profitable contracts Prevent loss masking
Immediate Loss Recognition Recognize loss in P&L immediately Timely loss reporting
CSM Zeroization Set CSM to zero No profit deferral for loss-making contracts
Liability Recognition Record full expected cost as liability Accurate obligation measurement
Loss Component Creation Establish loss component tracking Monitor loss recovery
Subsequent Measurement Treatment
Scenario Action Outcome
Contracts Become Onerous Use remaining CSM first, then recognize loss in P&L Gradual loss absorption
Increasing Onerosity Increase loss component Track additional losses
Assumption Improvements Reduce loss component Reflect favorable changes
Full Loss Recovery Clear loss component before CSM increase Ensure proper profit sequencing

8.8 Critical Implementation Notes

Permanent Classification: Once classified as onerous, contracts remain in separate groups
CSM Constraint: No CSM can be established while loss component exists
Proportional Allocation: Assumption changes allocated between loss component and remaining liability
Historical Benchmarking: Combined ratio analysis includes previous cohort performance
Continuous Monitoring: Regular assessment required throughout contract lifetime

8.9 Conclusion: Mastering Onerous Contract Treatment

Module 8 establishes the comprehensive framework for identifying, measuring, and accounting for onerous contracts under IFRS 17. The systematic approach ensures timely loss recognition, proper tracking through loss components, and accurate financial reporting of loss-making insurance contracts.

Key Takeaways:
  • Onerous contracts require immediate loss recognition and separate grouping
  • Loss components track recognized losses and constrain CSM establishment
  • Combined ratio analysis provides objective assessment criteria
  • Assumption changes are proportionally allocated between loss components and remaining liability
  • Continuous monitoring and historical benchmarking are essential for accurate classification
